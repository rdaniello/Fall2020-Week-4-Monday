Individual Project

The National Weight Control Registry (http://nwcr.ws )is a research study run at the Weight Control and Diabetes Research Center ( http://weightresearch.org ) in Providence RI. To participate in the study individuals must have lost 30lbs of body weight and maintained this weight loss for at least one year. The is significant since most individuals are capable of successful initial weight loss but fail to maintain this weight loss long-term. Once enrolled participants complete an initial baseline assessment focused on demographic, medical history, eating habits and weight loss strategies used during the initial weight loss. Then annually for the next 10 years participants a shorter survey focused on current weight, health, and weight loss maintenance strategies.

Given the sociological and medical cost of obesity in the United States (most participants are from the United States) this project will explore the participant data in the National Weight Control Registry (NWCR). The proposal will explore the demographic data of the NWCR participants as well the strategies used by the successful weigh loss maintainers.

Group Project

The conventional wisdom is that the United States has become more politically divided in the past 5 years. The project will attempt to quantify and demonstrate this increased political divide. The approach will be a comparative analysis of data gathered in 2015 and 2020 that makes use of a variety of data visualization techniques. The datasets will be cleaned and filtered for responses of interest (demographic, geographic and political affiliation) and exported to .csv file format to be consumed by data visualization libraries.

The data is a pair of surveys conducted in 2015 and 2020 by the Pew Research Center. Each survey contains demographic, geographic and political/ideology affiliation question. The dataset from 2015 contains 4787 response sets, while the 2020 dataset contains 1504 response sets. These data sets should be large enough for the task being attempted. The project may not limit itself to these 2 datasets and may add more datasets, especially if earlier useful datasets can be found.

The objective will be to explore the increase in political divide by showing an increase in political affiliation (bar graph). Since the datasets contain demographic and geographic data for the respondents it will be possible to show where this divide has become more apparent (map) and in which demographic groups (sex, age, race, income, etc.) this increase has been most pronounced.

The final goal will a data visualization platform that explores the change in political divide in the United States since 2015. It will accomplish this making use data visualization objects. These objects include (but not limited to) scatterplots, bar graphs and maps.
